Subtheme: Zoning and Housing Supply

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What is 'single-family' like in reality? Are single-family zones homes for large families with children or small, older households?

    Motivation: In Exclusionary by Design, the author brings up the questions on "whether single-family-only zoning represents good and just policy overall" and how they lack flexibility to accommodate the changing need of residents. This had me thinking what the current single-family homes look like. Increasingly, single-families are small. On average, people are having less children than before, which makes what a single-family looks like now much different from before. What are the families like now that are living in the single-family-only zones vs. other zones?

  2. Does single-family zoning create racially and economically segregated communities?

    3. Motivation: The Illegal City of Somerville highlights how zoning codes can make it effectively illegal to build the kind of dense, walkable neighborhoods that already exist and that many people want to live in. In addition, Exclusionary by Design traces how Greater Boston's zoning history has been shaped by both class elitism and racial exclusion, arguing that single-family requirements and large-lot mandates have restricted access to opportunity-rich neighborhoods by pricing out lower-income households, who are disproportionately people of color. Does our data show this pattern in Metro Boston? How bad is it?

  3. Does single-family zoning allocate land efficiently, or does it concentrate population into a fraction of available space?

    4. Motivation: Living in Cambridge, I've always felt the contrast between the spacious single-family neighborhoods in surrounding suburbs and the dense, packed apartment areas closer to the city. It always made me wonder whether this is just a natural urban-to-suburban thing or something actively created by zoning: are single-family zones deliberately reserving more land than their population needs, while everyone else competes for whatever's left?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Through this analysis, I explored three questions about single-family zoning in Metro Boston, working within a dataset that required significant quality caveats, including unreliable raw population counts, within-dataset household discrepancies (between 2010 and 2014-2018), and geographic gaps that exclude some of the region's densest urban cores. Despite these limitations, comparing between zone types rather than across municipalities revealed consistent patterns. 1) Single-family zones do not house distinctly different families: family sizes, age distributions, and household structures are nearly identical across SF and Other zones, challenging the argument that single-family zoning exists to protect large families with children. 2) While household structure looks the same, the people are different: SF zones are slightly whiter and wealthier, with every nonwhite group and every lower-income bracket underrepresented, suggesting that single-family zoning might sort residents by race and income rather than by family need. 3) This comes at a spatial cost: 74% of residential land is reserved for single-family zones that house only 53% of the population, compressing everyone else into a fraction of the remaining space, a pattern most extreme in inner suburbs like Milton, where 98% of land serves just 15% of residents. Taken together, these findings suggest that single-family zoning in Metro Boston functions less as family protection and more as a mechanism that concentrates land, wealth, and racial homogeneity while crowding the remaining population into increasingly dense alternatives. HOWEVER, these conclusions should be interpreted with caution given the dataset's geographic gaps, which likely underrepresent the region's most diverse and dense communities, meaning the true extent of these disparities may be larger than what this analysis captures.
